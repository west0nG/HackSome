# Reason as the buyer

Use one evidence-supported buyer in a real trigger situation. Follow what that
person can actually see and do rather than viewing the offer from inside the
company.

Examine the path that matters in this case: how the buyer encounters the offer,
understands it, compares it with doing nothing and other alternatives, decides
whether to trust the claim, accepts the risk and friction, and pays now. Include
post-purchase expectations when they affect belief, reviews, referrals, repeat
purchase, or another path to revenue.

Ground each important belief in observed behavior or source evidence. Mark
inference and unknowns plainly. Role-play can reveal friction, but the fact that
the model can imagine a buyer saying “yes” is never market evidence.

Notice mismatches between the buyer and the business form: a product can solve a
real problem yet be invisible, hard to trust, awkward to buy, or inferior to a
free/manual alternative in that situation. Conversely, do not demand proof of a
final user outcome before any sale unless that proof is load-bearing to the
buyer's decision or the business's continuation.

Return the buyer-side reasoning and its weakest assumptions. Do not prescribe a
universal journey, assign a conversion score, or approve the strategy.
