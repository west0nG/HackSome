# Coming up with something to pursue

When an objective decision is required but no candidate direction is in hand,
generate a few grounded options instead of making one up.

## Don't invent one out of thin air

Your default, asked to "think of a direction," is to produce something that *sounds*
plausible — a tidy "an AI-powered X for Y." That is the trap. A direction with no real
signal behind it is just a guess and cannot pass objective review. So don't
free-associate a direction — do the steps below.

## You are not limited to building products

A zero-human company can make money in more than one **business form**. You can pursue:

| Business form | Pursuing it means |
| --- | --- |
| A service / info-product | producing and delivering text/content/analysis — a report, a newsletter, writing done for someone, a course |
| A content / media play | producing content, publishing it, and growing an audience |
| A software product | building and shipping software |
| (Cross-border commerce) | sourcing, listing, running ads — later |

Treat the form as a **working hypothesis**, not an answer chosen independently of
the need. Start from forms the company can execute well; that capability fit is a
useful prior, and picking one form for a bounded research pass can reduce the
search space. Do not turn the prior into a lock.

As real user, channel, trust, and buying evidence arrives, ask whether the form
still fits the opportunity. A need may only become commercially reachable through
a different delivery or distribution shape; changing form can also change the
offer itself. Explore another form or reshape the candidate when the evidence
breaks the original coupling. Do not invent a universal metric to rank forms.
Whatever you explore, aim at something a department could actually build or
deliver.

## Get real signal before you imagine anything

Once you've picked a form, **don't dream up an opportunity** — go find a real
one. When no Company Objective is active, no Department can exist yet, so the
CEO must perform this bounded strategic research with its available research
tools. This is part of choosing company direction, not an ordinary Goal.

Once a Strategy Department exists, it may use this method inside its own
Department Objective. It turns evidence gaps into concrete Goals for Workers,
synthesizes verified results into a current candidate report under `/company`,
and leaves the Company Objective decision to the CEO. A CEO considering a real
revision should use `manage-objectives` to give a Department a durable evidence
responsibility. The Department—not the CEO—will turn it into concrete Goals.
Never use an old messaging or Goal CLI to bypass the three-layer ownership
boundary.

Build candidates from observed behavior and sources, not from imagination.

> **If you picked the service / info-product form, follow its concrete step-by-step
> method instead of the general approach above — read
> `references/finding-info-product-opportunities.md`.** (It routes you on to a
> per-marketplace playbook — Gumroad, Amazon KDP, or Etsy.) The other forms don't have
> a written method yet; for them, use the general "get real signal first" approach here.

## Produce a few real options, then hand them off

- Come up with **2-3 candidate directions that are genuinely different** — don't stop at
  the first one, and make at least one deliberately unlike the others. Write each so it
  stands on its own: who it's for, what it does, why now, and what signal says it has legs.
- **You only generate inside this skill.** Don't run a review or call BUILD,
  RESHAPE, or DROP. If you are the Strategy Department, persist the candidate
  set and its evidence under `/company`; if you are the CEO, return it to the
  current strategic reasoning. The CEO alone chooses and develops a candidate
  through `manage-objectives`, after which an independent ephemeral Verifier
  judges the proposal.

## Two guardrails

- Before you generate, check the company's memory at `/company` for directions already
  tried or rejected — don't resurface them.
- Aim small and real: something a department could deliver in days, matched to what the
  company can actually build — not a grand vision.
