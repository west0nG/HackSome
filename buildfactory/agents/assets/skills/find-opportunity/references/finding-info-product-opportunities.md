# Finding an info-product opportunity

You picked the **service / info-product** form. This file is how you find a real,
small, money-making **info-product** — a packaged piece of knowledge sold for money:
a focused guide, an ebook, a paid newsletter issue, a template, a checklist, a
planner, a mini-course. (The other shapes of this form — done-for-you service work,
research-as-a-service — don't have a written method yet; stick to info-products.)

## The one thing to hold onto

You can write anything. So the hard part is **not** "can we make it" — you can. The
only hard part is **"will anyone actually pay for it."** That flips where you spend
your effort: don't polish an idea, go find proof that **money is already moving**.
People asking, upvoting, or saying "great idea" is not proof. Someone having *paid*
is. Everything below is a way to perform bounded strategic research and read it
so you keep only the opportunities where money already changes hands and
there's still a gap you can fill. During Company Objective cold start the CEO
performs this research with its available tools; after a Research Department
exists, the CEO can assign a durable evidence responsibility through that
Department's Objective and let the Department create its own concrete Goals.

## The steps

**0. Don't resurface a dead direction.** Before anything, check the company's memory
at `/company` for directions already tried or rejected. Don't regenerate them.

**1. Find where this market already talks — and make sure you can read it.** List
the most active places a specific audience gathers and talks candidly
with each other (subreddits, forums, communities, Q&A sites), and whether each is
publicly readable without login. **Gate:** if fewer than ~2 are active *and* publicly
readable, you can't observe this market — pick a different one. You can only work from
what you can actually read.

**2. Harvest the raw complaints, at scale, in their own words.** Collect ~40–60
**verbatim** quotes of problems, frustrations, questions, and manual
workarounds — each with its source URL. Insist on raw quotes, not a summary or a tidy
conclusion; if the researcher hands back a summary, send it back. The corpus of real
words is the deliverable, not someone's interpretation of it. (This is your stand-in
for a human spending days immersed in a community.)

**3. Find the pains that are sharp and recurring.** Read the corpus yourself. A quote
is a real pain only if it carries a trigger word ("finally", "I hate", "I wish",
"annoying", "relief", "easier") or is someone actively asking for help. Then keep only
pains voiced **by several different people (roughly ≥5) across more than one place** —
recurrence is the signal, not one vivid post. Don't lock onto the first one you like.

**4. Prove people already PAY — this is the main gate.** A real, recurring pain is
still not a business until money moves. Sort every signal into three tiers and act
only on the top one:
- **Paid** (counts): someone bought, pre-ordered, or subscribes; a paid competitor
  that's clearly still in business; "take my money" / "is there a paid version".
- **Interest** (weak, needs paid backup): waitlists, search volume, big communities.
- **Talk** (ignore for a go decision): likes, upvotes, "great idea".

Require real paid evidence — roughly **≥3 independent instances** of money moving —
before a pain graduates to a candidate. If a pain is real but *nobody pays for
anything adjacent*, drop it: a pain nobody pays to fix is a hobby, not a product. And
note the counter-instinct: **existing paid competitors are good news** (proven
demand), not a reason to back off; "no one selling anything" usually means no one will
pay, not open water.

→ *Where and how you read "already paid" depends on the marketplace.* Pick the
platform a product like this would sell on (see **Platforms** below) and open its file
— it tells you exactly what to look at (sales counts, review counts, rankings) and the
numbers that separate proven from unproven.

**5. Check it isn't saturated — and find your wedge.** Proven demand is necessary, not
sufficient. Drop it if the market is **saturated**: the top products are all excellent,
cheap, and carry hundreds of reviews, with no recurring "I wish it had…" complaints —
there's no room. What you want is the narrow window of **"proven but beatable"**: real
sales are happening, *but* the leading products are mediocre, outdated, thin, or draw
recurring complaints. That specific complaint about what people already buy is your
wedge — the thing your version does better/narrower/more current.

→ *What "saturated" vs "beatable" looks like is platform-specific too* — the platform
file gives you the concrete read.

**6. Define 2–3 candidates — genuinely different from each other.** For each, name:
one **specific buyer** (a role/situation, not "marketers"), the **one burning pain** in
their words, and the **smallest text deliverable that fully removes it** (a focused
20-page guide, one template pack, one checklist — not "a complete course on <broad
topic>"). Each must be something **you can author end-to-end** with no heavy external
machinery (no video production, no live cohort, no custom software). Make at least one
deliberately unlike the others. Keep them small and real — not a grand vision.

**7. Return them to objective judgment — don't judge them inside this method.**
Don't call BUILD, RESHAPE, or DROP and don't run a review here. Return the
candidates to the CEO's strategic reasoning; develop the selected candidate
through `manage-objectives`, where an independent Verifier judges it.

## Platforms

Steps 4–5 happen on a real marketplace. Pick where the product would sell, then read
that platform's file for the concrete signals and thresholds — each platform is
different (where demand shows, what a beatable gap looks like, its quirks).

| Platform | Fits when | File |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Gumroad** | Digital downloads: guides, ebooks, Notion templates, prompt packs, swipe files. Lowest friction. | `references/platform-gumroad.md` |
| **Amazon KDP** | Kindle ebooks and low-content books (niche how-to, planners, journals, workbooks). Clearest demand signals. | `references/platform-amazon-kdp.md` |
| **Etsy** | Digital printables/templates: planners, trackers, worksheets, spreadsheets, resume templates. | `references/platform-etsy.md` |
| Substack | Paid newsletter (recurring). Needs audience-building — heavier. | *(playbook not written yet)* |
| Udemy | Courses. Video-heavy — weak fit for a text-native author. | *(playbook not written yet)* |

Early on, don't agonize over which platform — pick the one that fits the deliverable
shape and go. You'll try others over time.

## A note on the numbers

Every threshold in these files (how many quotes, how many sellers, what sales rank,
how many reviews) is an order-of-magnitude guide, not a hard rule — read it as "about"
and use judgment.
