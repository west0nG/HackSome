# Finding an info-product opportunity on Gumroad

Read this once you've picked Gumroad as where the product would sell. It makes steps
4–5 of the main method concrete for Gumroad. Gumroad fits digital downloads a
text-native author can produce: guides, ebooks, Notion templates, prompt packs, swipe
files, playbooks, planners.

## The fact that shapes everything here

**Gumroad barely has organic discovery.** Its Discover feed drives only ~10–20% of
sales; the other ~90% comes from the creator's own traffic. So you can't judge demand
by "is it hot inside Gumroad" alone — **external demand (search, communities, social)
is the real fuel**, and it's also your independent check on whether the need is real.
And since you have no audience of your own, always ask: could a buyer actually *reach*
this product — through Discover pull or through SEO content a department could write?

## Step 4 here — prove people already pay (Gumroad reads)

Only buyers can leave a rating, so **number of ratings is a floor on how many people
paid**. Sales counts show on only ~10% of product pages, so lean on ratings plus the
third-party trackers **gumtrends.com** and **profitable.app/gumroad** (they estimate
sales/revenue for products that don't display it).

Research prompt:
```text
Research question: "On Gumroad, find PAID products for the exact narrow topic '<topic>'. Search gumroad.com/discover
  (relevant tag + category) AND the trackers gumtrends.com and profitable.app/gumroad. For the top ~10 report:
  title, price, star rating, NUMBER OF RATINGS, sales count if shown, and third-party ESTIMATED sales/revenue
  where available. Exclude free and zero-rating products. Tell me how many candidates had NO ratings at all."
Expected evidence: "A table of paid products each with price + a real paid-demand proxy (ratings and/or a sales/revenue
  estimate), plus a count of how many had no ratings."
```
- **Keep** if ≥ ~3 sellers each show real paid evidence — roughly **≥20–50 ratings, or an estimated few-hundred sales**, priced around **$9–49**.
- **Drop** if only 0–1 products have any ratings and the rest are dead zero-sale listings. **Listing count lies** on Gumroad — the trackers show ~4.2M products listed but only ~478.6K with any sales or ratings. Count only what has real ratings/sales.

Also scan Gumroad's own **Best Sellers** vs **Hot & New / Rising Stars** for the topic
(the trackers expose these too): you want it selling in Best Sellers **and** at least
one newcomer from the last ~9 months gaining sales — proof demand is live *and* a new
entrant can still break in. If the top is all old products with no recent movers, the
niche is locked; skip.

## Step 5 here — saturation vs a beatable wedge (Gumroad reads)

gumtrends surfaces a **"% mixed reviews"** (2–4 star share) next to sales — use it.
```text
Research question: "For the top-SELLING Gumroad products on '<topic>' (use gumtrends.com's % mixed reviews / sales /
  revenue): find products with HIGH sales but a MEDIOCRE quality signal — rating ≤ ~4.3, a notable share of
  2-4 star reviews, thin or outdated content. Read their reviews and quote recurring complaints and 'I wish it
  had…' requests. Separately, estimate EXTERNAL demand (Google Trends interest, active search/discussion) and
  count how many competitors have ANY real ratings. Flag if the topic is a generic red-ocean like plain
  'Notion templates' or 'ChatGPT prompts'."
Expected evidence: "Top sellers with sales + rating + % mixed reviews + quoted complaints; an external-demand read; a
  count of competitors with real ratings; and a saturated-vs-winnable verdict."
```
- **Keep (proven but beatable):** a selling incumbent (real ratings/sales) that's mediocre — rating ≤ ~4.3, high % mixed reviews, thin or dated content, recurring complaints you could fix. High external demand + weak/mediocre on-Gumroad supply is the sweet spot.
- **Drop (saturated):** the top products are uniformly ≥4.7★ with hundreds of ratings at a low price and no unmet complaints. Remember you're not up against "N products" — you're up against N established businesses with email lists and affiliates while you start from zero reach.
- **Drop (no external demand):** looks OK on-Gumroad but nobody searches/discusses it externally — with no organic discovery, there's no traffic to convert.
- **Red flag:** generic "Notion templates" / "ChatGPT prompts" are already saturated. Go hyper-specific.

## Shape, price, and reachability

- **Keep shapes:** mini-guide/ebook ($10–49), Notion template ($9–29, bundles $35–79), prompt pack/library, swipe file, industry playbook, planner/printable — all authorable by you, and all under the $100 Discover ceiling.
- **Drop shapes:** courses (average ~115 sales and need an existing audience; often video), software/micro-SaaS (engineering + support).
- **Drop pricing:** $1–5 — fees eat it (a $1 sale nets about $0.05). Sit in the ~$19–49 impulse band.
- **Reachability gate:** keep topics a buyer can reach via SEO content (a department can write it) or that show Discover pull; drop topics whose sellers clearly run on a personal audience you don't have.

## Gumroad quirks to remember

- **Star ratings round up** (a 4.37 can show as 5★) and reviews are sorted positive-first — never trust the headline star number; make the researcher dig out the 2–4 star reviews.
- Third-party sales estimates are estimates — good as ordinal proxies, not exact truth.
- Fees and Discover rules shift over time; treat any specific % or price ceiling as "about", and re-check before relying on an exact figure.
